# Key Findings — Pharma Payments to Doctors

## The Single Most Important Number
**Doctors who received pharma payments prescribed 64.1% more of those companies' drugs.**

## Supporting Evidence

### Statistical Significance
- Mann-Whitney U test: p < 0.001 (cannot be due to chance)
- Sample: 6,300 doctors tracked across 3 years

### Dose-Response (Strongest Evidence of Relationship)
The more a doctor was paid, the more they prescribed:
- No payment → baseline
- Under $1k → +22%
- $1k to $10k → +41%
- Over $10k → **+170% (2.7x more)**

This dose-response pattern is the strongest indicator of a non-random relationship.

### Correlation
- Spearman ρ = 0.134, p < 0.001
- Weak correlation coefficient but extremely significant at this sample size

## Important Caveats
This analysis shows **association, not causation**. Possible explanations:
1. Pharma targets high-volume prescribers with payments (reverse causation)
2. Both driven by specialty type (confounding)
3. Payments genuinely influence prescribing behavior

## Data Source
CMS Open Payments + Medicare Part D (public domain government data)
Both datasets publicly available at data.cms.gov
